1Overview

A national entitlement service, rebuilt from the system up

The Eligibility Checking Engine (ECE) is the national digital infrastructure that determines, in real time, whether a child or family qualifies for Free School Meals, the Universal Infant Free School Meal entitlement, or 15 and 30-hour funded childcare across England and Wales. It performs millions of eligibility checks every year — underpinning a system worth billions in public benefit annually.

When I joined the programme in 2021, the service existed as a patchwork of legacy systems, manual processes, and data flows that had never been designed as a whole. Schools and local authorities were spending significant administrative resource managing a process that should have been near-invisible. Families were being turned away from entitlements they legally held, because the infrastructure to verify them reliably did not exist.

This case study describes how I led the service design work that defined a new system architecture, operating model, and end-to-end service — from discovery through to live — across one of the most policy-sensitive transformation programmes in recent DfE history.

2Context & Scale

The policy environment, the users, and the complexity underneath

Eligibility for Free School Meals and funded childcare is determined by household benefit status — cross-referencing HMRC and DWP benefit records against qualifying criteria set out in multiple policy frameworks. The entitlement is real and consequential: for families near the poverty threshold, it can represent hundreds of pounds of support each month. For the state, the accuracy of that determination matters both for fairness and for fiscal control.

The user ecosystem was unusually broad. Families applying for entitlements ranged from digitally confident parents to those in crisis with no stable internet access. Schools and nurseries — 16,500 across England and Wales — needed reliable, fast checking tools to manage admissions and meal registers. Local authorities carried oversight responsibilities and managed edge cases that the automated system could not resolve. DfE policy teams set the criteria, owned the legal framework, and needed the service to be able to flex as policy changed.

Layered underneath was a data architecture involving six government systems — HMRC PAYE, DWP Universal Credit, the National Benefits Database, the NINO index, and two internal DfE registers — none of which had been designed to work together in the ways the eligibility check required.

The policy environment added a further layer of complexity. Eligibility criteria were subject to change — sometimes at short notice — requiring a service architecture capable of absorbing policy variation without requiring a full redevelopment cycle. This tension between legislative rigidity and operational flexibility was one of the defining design constraints of the programme.

3Reframing the Problem

From "fix the portal" to redesigning the infrastructure of entitlement

The programme had been commissioned with a clear brief: modernise the school eligibility checking portal. The existing interface was outdated, difficult to use on mobile, and inaccessible for a significant proportion of families. The assumption was that a front-end redesign would resolve the core user problems.

Early discovery work challenged this framing directly. The user research found that the interface was rarely the point of failure — families who were eligible but not receiving their entitlement had typically been turned away before they ever reached the digital service, or had been incorrectly flagged as ineligible by a data mismatch that no school administrator had the tools to resolve. The problem was not a poor interface. It was a broken data ecosystem and an operating model that placed the burden of exception-handling on the least resourced part of the system: individual schools.

"The portal is the smallest part of the problem. What breaks the service happens three systems upstream — before any family ever sees a screen."

— Design brief to programme leadership, January 2022

Reframing the problem required sustained advocacy. The programme had a commercial structure, a technical roadmap, and a set of contractual deliverables — all oriented around the front-end product. Making the case for a systems-level redesign meant presenting evidence about where failures actually occurred, mapping the cost of unresolved exceptions to schools and local authorities, and demonstrating that a better portal would not materially improve outcomes without addressing the data plumbing underneath.

This reframe was accepted — and it shaped every subsequent design and technology decision on the programme. The ECE moved from being a front-end modernisation to becoming a data integration and operating model redesign, with the user-facing service as the visible layer of something much more structurally ambitious.

4Role & Leadership

Design leadership across a multi-supplier, policy-constrained programme

My role on the ECE programme was Principal Service Designer, embedded within a multi-supplier delivery team and working directly with DfE's digital, policy, and operations leads. I was responsible for the end-to-end service design: not just the family-facing application journey, but the school administrator tools, the local authority escalation model, and the internal operating procedures that sat behind the automated system.

Scope of responsibility

  • Led service design across all three user groups: families, schools, and local authorities
  • Defined the end-to-end service map and operating model blueprint
  • Directed user research strategy across discovery, alpha, and beta phases
  • Translated policy constraints into design requirements and data architecture implications
  • Chaired design reviews with DfE policy, legal, and delivery leadership
  • Advised on procurement and supplier design capability assessment
  • Led GDS service standard assessment preparation for beta and live

The leadership context was demanding. The programme involved four separate delivery suppliers, a DfE product owner with limited prior experience of GDS-standard service design, and a policy team that had not previously worked in an agile delivery environment. My role required operating simultaneously as a design practitioner, a design advocate, and — at times — as the person who translated between technical, policy, and operational realities that were not naturally in conversation with each other.

I also carried responsibility for design quality assurance across the programme: establishing design standards, reviewing work produced by other designers, and ensuring consistency across a service with multiple distinct touchpoints and user populations.

5Understanding the System

Mapping the ecosystem before designing within it

The first significant design artefact produced on the programme was not a prototype or a user journey — it was a system ecosystem map. The purpose was to make visible what had never previously been represented in a single place: the full network of actors, data flows, responsibilities, and dependencies that made up the eligibility system.

This map became the foundational design tool for the programme. It revealed where accountability was genuinely ambiguous — where a check could fail and no single organisation knew whose responsibility the resolution was. It exposed data flows that had been assumed to be reliable but were in practice inconsistent or time-delayed. And it identified the places where the system placed the greatest burden on users who had the least capacity to absorb it.

What the map made visible above all else was the exception problem. A significant proportion of checks produced an inconclusive or incorrect result — not because the family was ineligible, but because a name mismatch, a data lag, or a coding inconsistency in one of the upstream systems had corrupted the signal. These cases fell to school administrators to resolve manually. There was no standard process, no escalation path, and no data about how many were being resolved versus abandoned.

Quantifying the exception volume — and tracing it back to specific upstream failure modes — was one of the most important pieces of analytical work on the programme. It provided the evidence base for the operating model redesign that followed.

The blueprint produced three critical diagnostic insights. First, the inconclusive result — where the system returns neither a clear pass nor fail — was far more common than programme leadership had understood, and its downstream effects were almost entirely invisible to the programme team. Second, the resolution burden fell almost exclusively on schools, but schools had no tools, training, or mandate to resolve it reliably. Third, local authorities — theoretically the backstop for complex cases — had no defined role in the current operating model and were being contacted informally and inconsistently.

These three insights became the direct inputs for the operating model redesign and the exception routing architecture that followed in alpha.

7Critical Decisions

The choices that shaped the programme

01 Redesign the operating model before building the front end

Context

The programme brief prioritised the family-facing portal as the first delivery milestone. The commercial contract was structured around it. The programme director wanted something to show ministers within six months.

Tension

Discovery evidence showed that the portal would not materially improve outcomes without an exception routing model behind it. Building the front end first would lock in a broken operating model before it had been designed. Speed versus systemic effectiveness.

Outcome

Secured an extended alpha phase covering both the operating model and the front end in parallel. The exception routing architecture was defined and agreed before any production code was written. This decision prevented a significant rework cycle later in the programme.

02 Make the eligibility logic visible to schools

Context

The existing system returned a binary result code — pass or fail — with no explanation. School staff had no way to understand why a check had failed or what action, if any, was appropriate. DfE's default position was that the logic should remain opaque for data protection reasons.

Tension

Transparency versus data protection and policy exposure. Making the determination logic legible to schools risked surfacing information about household benefit status. But opacity was generating thousands of incorrectly declined applications. User need versus institutional risk aversion.

Outcome

Worked with DfE legal and policy teams to define a tiered transparency model — providing enough information for school administrators to identify the type of failure and take appropriate action, without exposing benefit-level detail. This became a core design pattern across the school-facing interface.

03 Design for policy change from the start

Context

The ECE covered three distinct entitlement types, each governed by separate legislation with different qualifying criteria. Policy teams anticipated at least two legislative changes during the service's operational life. The technology team had proposed a hardcoded rules engine that would require redevelopment for each policy change.

Tension

A configurable rules architecture was technically more complex and costly to build initially. The programme was under time and budget pressure. The argument that "we can refactor later" was politically easier to make. Short-term delivery versus long-term service sustainability.

Outcome

Developed a service design position paper on policy flexibility as a first-order requirement, backed by cost modelling of the alternative. DfE agreed to commission a configurable rules architecture. When eligibility criteria changed in 2023, the update was absorbed in days rather than months.

8Defining the Future State

What the service needed to become

The future state vision for the ECE was not defined as a set of features or interface designs — it was defined as a set of properties the service needed to have in order to be genuinely fit for purpose at national scale. These properties were arrived at through synthesis of discovery research, the ecosystem and blueprint analysis, and structured workshops with DfE policy, operations, and technology leadership.

The core vision: an eligibility determination infrastructure that is accurate by default, transparent in its operation, and resilient to the policy and operational changes it will inevitably face. A service that disappears into the background for the families and schools that use it — because it works reliably enough that it requires no attention.

1
Accuracy as a design requirement
Every design decision should be evaluated against its effect on determination accuracy. An interface that is easy to use but produces more incorrect outcomes is a failure. Accuracy is primary; usability is the means of achieving it at scale.
2
Exceptions are part of the service, not outside it
Every inconclusive result is a real family. The exception pathway must be as deliberately designed as the standard pathway — with clear ownership, routing, and resolution. Exceptions cannot be externalised to schools.
3
Transparency proportionate to responsibility
Each actor in the system should see exactly as much as they need to fulfil their role and no more. Schools need actionable guidance, not raw data. Families need a plain-language outcome, not eligibility logic.
4
Policy flexibility as infrastructure
Eligibility criteria will change. The service architecture must absorb policy variation without requiring a redevelopment cycle. Rules configuration is a service design requirement, not a technical nice-to-have.
5
Designed for the hardest cases first
The families least able to navigate a complex system are those with the most to lose from a service failure. Design from the edges in. If it works for a family in crisis with inconsistent benefit records, it works for everyone.
6
Operational sustainability over delivery speed
A service that is fast to build but expensive to operate and maintain is not a good service. Design decisions must account for the operational model that will run this service at scale, after the programme team has moved on.
9Service Transformation

What actually changed — in operations, experience, and model

The transformation was not principally visible in the user interface. The most consequential changes were structural: to the data architecture, the operating model, and the distribution of responsibility across the system. The front end was improved significantly — it became accessible, mobile-first, and plain-language throughout — but that improvement was only meaningful because the infrastructure behind it had been redesigned.

10Innovation & Differentiation

What made this work distinctive

Most eligibility digitisation programmes in government focus on replacing a form with a digital form. The ECE programme was distinctive because it treated eligibility checking as a systems design problem rather than a transactional interface problem. The innovation was primarily architectural and organisational — not technological.

The configurable rules engine was the most technically novel element. Rather than encoding eligibility logic in application code — the standard approach — the programme designed a rules layer that could be modified through a governed configuration interface, without requiring engineering change. This was a direct product of the service design position paper on policy flexibility, and it represented a meaningful departure from how DfE had previously approached benefits-adjacent systems.

The exception routing model was the most operationally innovative element. No prior DfE eligibility service had designed for the exception pathway as a first-class component of the operating model. Defining the exception as a service state with its own user needs, routing logic, and resolution SLAs required a significant renegotiation of how DfE, local authorities, and schools understood their respective responsibilities — and what the service needed to support in each case.

The tiered transparency design — where the level of information shared with each actor was calibrated to their decision-making need rather than defaulting to minimum disclosure — provided a practical model for how government services can be transparent without being legally or operationally exposed. This has since been referenced by other DfE programmes facing similar data-sharing constraints.

11Measurable Impact

Outcomes that can be evidenced

  • Millions of eligibility checks performed annually through a redesigned engine that is more accurate, faster, and operationally sustainable than its predecessor — serving 2.2 million children's Free School Meals entitlements and 466,000 funded childcare families.
  • Significant reduction in incorrectly declined applications following introduction of the tiered result model and exception routing — with pilot data showing materially fewer families turning up to schools to find their entitlement had not been processed.
  • Policy update absorbed in days rather than months following the 2023 eligibility criteria change — the first real-world test of the configurable rules architecture, which operated as designed with no engineering intervention required.
  • Passed GDS beta and live assessment with no conditions — the service standard assessment panel specifically commended the exception model design and the approach to data transparency as examples of good practice for other DfE programmes.
  • IESE Gold Award for Best Transformation Team, 2026 — the assessment panel cited the systems approach to service design and the operating model innovation as primary reasons for the award.
  • National Technology Awards finalist, 2026 — recognised as Technology Project of the Year for the scale and complexity of the data integration challenge resolved by the programme.
  • Operational dashboard adopted as a live management tool by DfE operations, providing for the first time a programme-level view of eligibility system performance — including exception rates, resolution times, and determination accuracy indicators.
12Organisational Influence

How this changed thinking beyond the programme

The ECE's influence on DfE's approach to service design extended well beyond the eligibility checking domain. Several of the design approaches developed on the programme were subsequently adopted — in modified form — by other DfE digital programmes.

The tiered transparency design pattern — calibrating information disclosure to role and decision-making need rather than defaulting to minimum — was referenced explicitly in two subsequent DfE programmes facing analogous data-sharing constraints. It provided a practical precedent for how to navigate the tension between data protection conservatism and the operational need for actionable information.

The exception-as-first-class-service-state framing influenced how DfE's digital team wrote subsequent service standards guidance. Prior to ECE, exception handling was consistently described in DfE programme documentation as an "out of scope" or "phase two" consideration. Post-ECE, the expectation that exception pathways must be designed as part of the core service — not bolted on afterwards — became part of the internal design standards conversation.

The programme also had a direct influence on how DfE commissioned service design work. The case for extended discovery and operating model work before front-end development — made and won on ECE — was cited in subsequent procurement decisions as justification for including operating model design in service design scopes. This is a small but meaningful shift in how the organisation values and positions design expertise within complex transformation programmes.

"The ECE showed us what it looks like when service design operates at the infrastructure level rather than the interface level. That's the model we want to replicate."

— DfE Digital Director, programme retrospective, December 2023
13Reflection

What I learned. What I would do differently.

1

The reframe is the hardest design act

The most consequential design decision on this programme happened before any design work began — the decision to challenge the brief and reframe the problem. This required evidence, persistence, and a willingness to create short-term friction with programme leadership. I would do it again, and I would do it faster. The instinct to validate the brief before accepting it is one of the most valuable things a senior designer brings to a programme, and it is chronically undervalued in commercial delivery contexts.

2

Involve local authorities earlier

The local authority exception routing model was one of the programme's most important design innovations — but it was developed too late in alpha, after the initial architecture had already been defined. LAs were brought into the co-design process as a correction rather than from the start. Earlier engagement would have produced a more refined model and reduced the rework required in beta. In services with complex multi-tier actor models, every tier needs research from the beginning.

3

Make the exception data visible from day one

The operational dashboard was designed and commissioned in beta — but the data it surfaced should have been captured from the start of alpha. The programme made significant decisions about exception volume and distribution on the basis of incomplete data, because the instrumentation to measure it didn't exist yet. Building observability into the service from the earliest stages is a design responsibility, not a technology afterthought.

4

Design influence requires presence in the rooms where decisions are made

The decisions that most shaped the ECE — the extended alpha, the configurable rules engine, the transparency model — were not made in design reviews. They were made in programme boards, procurement conversations, and DfE leadership sessions. Being physically present in those rooms, with evidence and a clear position, was as important as any design artefact. Service design at this level is a political as much as a craft practice.
